# AMC12 2013-A Q16

![[assets/problems/2013-A/Q16.png]]

## Problem

$A$, $B$, and $C$ are three piles of rocks. The mean weight in $A$ is $40$ pounds, the mean weight in $B$ is $50$ pounds, the mean weight in combined piles $A$ and $B$ is $43$ pounds, and the mean weight in combined piles $A$ and $C$ is $44$ pounds. What is the greatest possible integer value for the mean weight in combined piles $B$ and $C$?

## Answer

**E.** $59$

## Solution

Let the numbers of rocks in $A$, $B$, and $C$ be $a$, $b$, and $c$. From the mean of $A$ and $B$,
$$\frac{40a+50b}{a+b}=43,$$
so $7b=3a$. Write
$$a=7k,\qquad b=3k.$$

Let the mean of pile $C$ be $\mu_C$. The mean of $A$ and $C$ gives
$$\frac{40(7k)+\mu_Cc}{7k+c}=44,$$
so
$$\mu_Cc=28k+44c.$$
Therefore the mean $\mu$ of $B$ and $C$ is
$$\mu=\frac{50(3k)+28k+44c}{3k+c}=\frac{178k+44c}{3k+c}.$$

Since $k,c>0$,
$$\mu<\frac{178}{3}=59\frac13.$$
Thus the greatest possible integer is at most $59$. It is attainable: choose $k=15$ and $c=1$, which gives $\mu=59$. Hence the answer is $59$.

## Knowledge points

- Weighted averages
- Integer ratios
- Optimization by bounds

## Key idea

Use the first combined mean to determine the ratio of the pile sizes, then bound the final weighted mean.

## Common mistakes

- Averaging $40$, $50$, and $44$ directly
- Forgetting that pile sizes are positive integers

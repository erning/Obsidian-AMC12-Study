# AMC12 2013-A Q24

![[assets/problems/2013-A/Q24.png]]

## Problem

Three distinct segments are chosen at random from the segments whose endpoints are vertices of a regular $12$-gon. What is the probability that their lengths are the three side lengths of a triangle with positive area?

## Answer

**E.** $\frac{223}{286}$

## Solution

Place the regular $12$-gon on a unit circle. A chord joining vertices $k$ steps apart has length
$$d_k=2\sin(15k^\circ),\qquad1\le k\le6.$$
There are $12$ chords of each length $d_1$ through $d_5$, and $6$ diameters of length $d_6$.

The triangle inequality fails precisely for the following nondecreasing index triples:
$$
\begin{gathered}
(1,1,3),(1,1,4),(1,1,5),(1,1,6),\\
(1,2,4),(1,2,5),(1,2,6),(1,3,5),(1,3,6),(2,2,6).
\end{gathered}
$$
Using multiplicities $(12,12,12,12,12,6)$, these ten cases account for
$$10080$$
choices. The total number of choices is
$$\binom{66}{3}=45760.$$
Thus the desired probability is
$$1-\frac{10080}{45760}=1-\frac{63}{286}=\frac{223}{286}.$$

## Knowledge points

- Regular polygon chord lengths
- Triangle inequality
- Counting with multiplicities

## Key idea

Classify chords by their step distance around the polygon, then count the index triples that violate the largest-side inequality.

## Common mistakes

- Treating the $6$ diameters as if there were $12$
- Including degenerate triangles as positive-area triangles

# AMC12 2013-A Q25

![[assets/problems/2013-A/Q25.png]]

## Problem

Let $f:\mathbb C\to\mathbb C$ be defined by
$$f(z)=z^2+iz+1.$$
How many complex numbers $z$ are there such that $\operatorname{Im}(z)>0$ and both the real and imaginary parts of $f(z)$ are integers with absolute value at most $10$?

## Answer

**A.** $399$

## Solution

On the upper half-plane, $f$ is one-to-one. Indeed, if $f(z_1)=f(z_2)$, then
$$
(z_1-z_2)(z_1+z_2+i)=0.
$$
The second factor cannot be zero when both imaginary parts are positive.

The image of the upper half-plane is
$$f(\{z:\operatorname{Im}z>0\})
=\{w:\operatorname{Re}(w)<(\operatorname{Im}(w))^2+1\}.$$
Therefore count integer points $w=a+ib$ with $|a|,|b|\le10$ satisfying
$$a<b^2+1.$$

There are $21^2=441$ integer points in the square. The excluded points have $a\ge b^2+1$, which is possible only for $|b|\le3$. Their number is
$$
\sum_{b=-3}^{3}(10-b^2)=42.
$$
Hence the number of valid points, and therefore valid $z$, is
$$441-42=399.$$

## Knowledge points

- Complex numbers
- Injective functions
- Integer-point counting

## Key idea

Count lattice points in the image of the upper half-plane instead of solving for every complex number separately.

## Common mistakes

- Counting all points in the $21\times21$ square
- Forgetting that the image inequality is strict

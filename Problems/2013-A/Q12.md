# AMC12 2013-A Q12

![[assets/problems/2013-A/Q12.png]]

## Problem

The angles in a particular triangle are in arithmetic progression, and the side lengths are $4$, $5$, and $x$. The sum of the possible values of $x$ equals $a+\sqrt b+\sqrt c$, where $a$, $b$, and $c$ are positive integers. What is $a+b+c$?

## Answer

**A.** $36$

## Solution

If the angles are $\alpha-\delta$, $\alpha$, and $\alpha+\delta$, then their sum gives $\alpha=60^\circ$. Thus one of the side lengths is opposite a $60^\circ$ angle.

If $x$ is opposite $60^\circ$, the Law of Cosines gives
$$x^2=4^2+5^2-2\cdot4\cdot5\cos60^\circ=21,$$
so $x=\sqrt{21}$.

If $5$ is opposite $60^\circ$, then
$$25=x^2-4x+16,$$
so $x=2+\sqrt{13}$. If $4$ is opposite $60^\circ$, the resulting quadratic has no real solution.

The sum of the possible values is
$$2+\sqrt{13}+\sqrt{21},$$
so
$$a+b+c=2+13+21=36.$$

## Knowledge points

- Arithmetic progression of angles
- Law of cosines
- Quadratic equations

## Key idea

The middle angle of three angles in arithmetic progression must be $60^\circ$.

## Common mistakes

- Assuming $x$ must be the largest side
- Missing the cases where $4$ or $5$ is opposite the $60^\circ$ angle

# AMC12 2012-A Q15

![[assets/problems/2012-A/Q15.png]]

## Problem

A $3\times3$ square is partitioned into unit squares, each independently painted white or black with equal probability. The square is rotated $90^\circ$ clockwise, and every white square that moves into a position formerly occupied by a black square is painted black. What is the probability that the grid is entirely black?

## Answer

**A.** $\frac{49}{512}$

## Solution

Under a $90^\circ$ rotation, the four corners form one $4$-cycle, the four edge squares form another $4$-cycle, and the center is fixed. These groups are independent.

For a $4$-cycle, direct inspection of its $2^4=16$ colorings shows that $7$ result in all four positions being black after the operation. Thus each cycle succeeds with probability $\frac7{16}$. The center must initially be black, with probability $\frac12$.

Therefore
$$\frac12\left(\frac7{16}\right)^2=\frac{49}{512}.$$

## Knowledge points

- Independent probability
- Symmetry and cycles
- Casework

## Key idea

Decompose the grid into independent rotation orbits.

## Common mistakes

- Treating the nine squares as one undifferentiated case
- Forgetting that the center does not move

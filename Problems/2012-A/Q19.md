# AMC12 2012-A Q19

![[assets/problems/2012-A/Q19.png]]

## Problem

Six people—Adam, Benin, Chiang, Deshawn, Esther, and Fiona—have internet accounts. Some, but not all, pairs are friends, and everyone has the same number of friends. In how many different ways can this happen?

## Answer

**B.** $170$

## Solution

Model the friendships as a simple graph on six labeled vertices. The common degree can be $1,2,3,$ or $4$. Complementary graphs pair degree $d$ with degree $5-d$.

For degree $1$, the graph is a perfect matching, giving
$$5\cdot3=15$$
graphs. Degree $4$ also gives $15$ by complementation.

For degree $2$, the graph is either two triangles, counted by
$$\frac12\binom63=10,$$
or one $6$-cycle, counted by
$$\frac{6!}{12}=60.$$
Thus degree $2$ gives $70$, and degree $3$ also gives $70$ by complementation.

The total is
$$15+15+70+70=170.$$

## Knowledge points

- Regular graphs
- Complementary counting
- Cycles and matchings

## Key idea

Classify the possible common degrees and use graph complementation to halve the work.

## Common mistakes

- Counting unlabeled graphs instead of labeled people
- Forgetting the degree-$4$ and degree-$3$ complements

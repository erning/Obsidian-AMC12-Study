# AMC12 2018-A Q23

![[assets/problems/2018-A/Q23.png]]

## Problem

In $\triangle PAT$, $\angle P=36^\circ$, $\angle A=56^\circ$, and $PA=10$. Points $U,G$ lie on $TP,TA$ with $PU=AG=1$. If $M,N$ are the midpoints of $PA,UG$, what is the acute angle between $MN$ and $PA$?

## Answer

**E.** $80^\circ$

## Solution

Extend $PN$ beyond $N$ to $Q$ with $PN=NQ$. Since $N$ is the midpoint of both $UG$ and $PQ$, quadrilateral $UPGQ$ is a parallelogram. Hence $GQ\parallel PT$ and $GQ=PU=1=AG$, so $\triangle QGA$ is isosceles.

The angle at $G$ is

$$\angle QGA=180^\circ-\angle T=\angle P+\angle A=92^\circ,$$

so $\angle QAG=44^\circ$. In $\triangle QPA$, $M,N$ are midpoints, so $MN\parallel AQ$. Therefore the angle between $MN$ and $PA$ is

$$180^\circ-(44^\circ+56^\circ)=80^\circ.$$

## Knowledge points

- Midline theorem
- Parallelograms and isosceles triangles

## Key idea

Create a parallelogram so that the midpoint line becomes parallel to a computable segment.

## Common mistakes

- Reporting the obtuse angle $100^\circ$ instead of the acute angle.
- Assuming $MN$ is parallel to $UG$ rather than to $AQ$.

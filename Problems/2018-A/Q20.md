# AMC12 2018-A Q20

![[assets/problems/2018-A/Q20.png]]

## Problem

Triangle $ABC$ is isosceles right with $AB=AC=3$. $M$ is the midpoint of hypotenuse $BC$. Points $I,E$ lie on $AC,AB$, respectively, with $AI>AE$, and $AIME$ is cyclic. If triangle $EMI$ has area 2, write

$$CI=\frac{a-\sqrt b}{c}$$

with $b$ squarefree. Find $a+b+c$.

## Answer

**D.** $12$

## Solution

Let $x=CI$ and $y=BE$. Since $\angle IAE=90^\circ$ and $AIME$ is cyclic, $\angle IME=90^\circ$. Applying the Law of Cosines in the two right triangles around $M$ gives

$$IM^2=x^2-3x+\frac92,\qquad ME^2=y^2-3y+\frac92.$$

The Pythagorean relation in $\triangle EMI$ and $\triangle IAE$ simplifies to $x+y=3$. Thus $y=3-x$ makes $IM^2=ME^2$. Since $[EMI]=2$,

$$\frac12IM\cdot ME=2,$$

so $IM^2=ME^2=4$. Hence

$$x^2-3x+\frac92=4,$$

or $x^2-3x+\frac12=0$. The condition $AI>AE$ gives $y>x$, so choose the smaller root:

$$x=\frac{3-\sqrt7}{2}.$$

Therefore $a+b+c=3+7+2=12$.

## Knowledge points

- Cyclic quadrilaterals
- Law of cosines
- Quadratic equations

## Key idea

Use cyclicity to create a right angle, then exploit the equal-length relation forced by the area.

## Common mistakes

- Choosing the larger quadratic root.
- Misreading the requested form as $a-\sqrt{b/c}$.

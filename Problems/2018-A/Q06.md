# AMC12 2018-A Q06

![[assets/problems/2018-A/Q06.png]]

## Problem

For positive integers $m,n$ with $m+10<n+1$, both the mean and median of

$$\{m,m+4,m+10,n+1,n+2,2n\}$$

equal $n$. Find $m+n$.

## Answer

**B.** $21$

## Solution

The given inequality puts the six numbers in increasing order. Therefore the median is the average of the third and fourth entries:

$$\frac{(m+10)+(n+1)}2=n,$$

so $m=n-11$. Substitute this into the mean condition:

$$\frac{(n-11)+(n-7)+(n-1)+(n+1)+(n+2)+2n}{6}=n.$$

This gives $7n-16=6n$, so $n=16$ and $m=5$. Hence $m+n=21$.

## Knowledge points

- Mean and median
- Ordered data

## Key idea

Use the provided inequality to identify the middle two entries.

## Common mistakes

- Averaging the wrong pair for the median.
- Forgetting to substitute $m=n-11$ into every term.

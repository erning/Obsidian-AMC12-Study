# AMC12 2018-A Q25

![[assets/problems/2018-A/Q25.png]]

## Problem

For nonzero digits $a,b,c$, let $A_n$ and $B_n$ be the $n$-digit numbers consisting entirely of $a$ and $b$, respectively, and let $C_n$ be the $2n$-digit number consisting entirely of $c$. What is the greatest $a+b+c$ for which $C_n-B_n=A_n^2$ holds for at least two values of $n$?

## Answer

**D.** $18$

## Solution

Let $R_n=(10^n-1)/9$. Then $A_n=aR_n$, $B_n=bR_n$, and $C_n=cR_{2n}$. Substituting and multiplying by $81/(10^n-1)$ gives

$$ (9c-a^2)10^n=9b-9c-a^2.$$

If this holds for two different $n$, the coefficient of $10^n$ must be zero, and then the constant term must also be zero. Hence

$$9c=a^2,\qquad b=2c.$$

Since $a$ is a digit, $a\in\{3,6,9\}$. The corresponding triples are $(3,2,1)$ and $(6,8,4)$; $a=9$ would give $b=18$, not a digit. The larger digit sum is

$$6+8+4=18.$$

## Knowledge points

- Repunit notation
- Polynomial identity in $10^n$

## Key idea

Require the equation to hold for two distinct exponents, forcing both coefficients to vanish.

## Common mistakes

- Treating $C_n$ as an $n$-digit number.
- Checking only one value of $n$.

# AMC12 2022-A Q17

![[assets/problems/2022-A/Q17.png]]

## Problem

Suppose $a$ is real and
$$
a\bigl(\sin x+\sin(2x)\bigr)=\sin(3x).
$$
The equation has more than one solution in $(0,\pi)$. The set of all such $a$ can be written as $(p,q)\cup(q,r)$, where $p<q<r$. What is $p+q+r$?

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(A) $-4$**

## Solution

Since $\sin x\ne0$ on $(0,\pi)$, divide by $\sin x$. Using
$$
\sin2x=2\sin x\cos x,\qquad
\frac{\sin3x}{\sin x}=4\cos^2x-1,
$$
we obtain
$$
a(1+2\cos x)=(2\cos x-1)(2\cos x+1).
$$
The value $\cos x=-\frac12$, or $x=\frac{2\pi}{3}$, always gives a solution because both sides before division vanish. For any other solution, cancel $1+2\cos x$:
$$
a=2\cos x-1,
\qquad
\cos x=\frac{a+1}{2}.
$$
This gives a solution in $(0,\pi)$ exactly when
$$
-1<\frac{a+1}{2}<1,
\qquad -3<a<1.
$$
The second solution is distinct from $\frac{2\pi}{3}$ unless $a=-2$. Hence
$$
a\in(-3,-2)\cup(-2,1),
$$
so
$$
p+q+r=-3-2+1=\boxed{-4}.
$$

- Factoring

## Key idea

Separate the always-present solution before canceling a factor.

## Common mistakes

- Canceling $1+2\cos x$ and losing the solution $x=\frac{2\pi}{3}$.

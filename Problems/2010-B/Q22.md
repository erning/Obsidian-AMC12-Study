# AMC12 2010-B Q22

![[assets/problems/2010-B/Q22.png]]

## Problem

Let $ABCD$ be a cyclic quadrilateral. The side lengths of $ABCD$ are distinct integers less than $15$ such that $BC\cdot CD=AB\cdot DA.$ What is the largest possible value of $BD?$

(A) $\frac{\sqrt{325}}{2}$
(B) $\sqrt{185}$
(C) $\frac{\sqrt{389}}{2}$
(D) $\frac{\sqrt{425}}{2}$
(E) $\frac{\sqrt{533}}{2}$

## Answer

**D.** $\frac{\sqrt{425}}{2}$

## Solution

Let $a=AB,$ $b=BC,$ $c=CD,$ $d=DA$ and $k=bc=ad.$ Writing each triangle's area in terms of the circumradius and using $[ABC]+[CDA]=$ $[BCD]+[ABD]$ gives $(ab+cd)\cdot AC=2k\cdot BD.$

Ptolemy's theorem gives $AC\cdot BD=ac+bd.$ Eliminating $AC,$ $$ \begin{aligned} BD^2 &=\frac{(ac+bd)(ab+cd)}{2k} \\ &=\frac12\left(a^2+b^2+c^2+d^2\right). \end{aligned} $$

The sides are distinct integers below $15$ with $bc=ad,$ so neither $11$ nor $13$ can appear (each is prime and would need a matching factor on the other side).

To maximize, take the largest side $14.$ Writing the others as $s_1\gt s_2\gt s_3$ with $14s_3=s_1s_2,$ the best case is $s_2=7,$ giving $s_1=2s_3$ and $(a,b,c,d)=(14,12,7,6).$ Then $$ \begin{aligned} 2BD^2 &=14^2+12^2+7^2+6^2 \\ &=425, \end{aligned} $$ so $BD=\sqrt{\frac{425}{2}}.$

Thus, the correct answer is **D**.

## Knowledge points

- Cyclic quadrilateral
- Ptolemy’s theorem
- Optimization

## Key idea

For a cyclic quadrilateral, use Ptolemy’s theorem to relate the diagonals and maximize the requested product under the side constraints.

## Common mistakes

- Applying a formula before identifying the relevant quantities and constraints.
- Counting invalid cases or overlooking a boundary condition from the statement.

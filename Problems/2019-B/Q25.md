# AMC12 2019-B Q25

![[assets/problems/2019-B/Q25.png]]

## Problem

Let $ABCD$ be a convex quadrilateral with $BC=2$ and $CD=6$. Suppose the centroids of $\triangle ABC$, $\triangle BCD$, and $\triangle ACD$ form an equilateral triangle. What is the maximum possible area of $ABCD$?

## Answer

**C.** $12+10\sqrt3$

## Solution

The pairwise differences of the three centroids are proportional to $A-D$, $B-A$, and $B-D$. Since the centroid triangle is equilateral, these vectors have equal lengths; hence $\triangle ABD$ is equilateral. Let its side be $s=BD$.

Splitting the quadrilateral along $BD$ gives

$$[ABCD]=\frac{\sqrt3}{4}s^2+\frac12(2)(6)\sin C.$$

By the Law of Cosines in $\triangle BCD$,

$$s^2=2^2+6^2-2(2)(6)\cos C=40-24\cos C.$$

Therefore

$$[ABCD]=10\sqrt3-6\sqrt3\cos C+6\sin C.$$

The last two terms have maximum $\sqrt{(6\sqrt3)^2+6^2}=12$, so the maximum area is $10\sqrt3+12$.

## Knowledge points

- Centroids and vectors
- Law of cosines
- Trigonometric optimization

## Key idea

Convert the centroid condition into an equilateral condition on $\triangle ABD$.

## Common mistakes

- Comparing centroid distances without multiplying by 3.
- Maximizing the two trigonometric terms independently.

# AMC12 2020-A Q11

![[assets/problems/2020-A/Q11.png]]

## Problem

A frog sitting at $(1,2)$ begins a sequence of jumps. Each jump is parallel to one of the coordinate axes and has length $1$, and the direction is chosen independently at random from up, down, right, and left. The sequence ends when the frog reaches a side of the square with vertices $(0,0),(0,4),(4,4),(4,0)$. What is the probability that the sequence ends on a vertical side?

## Answer

**B.** $\frac58$

## Solution

Let $P(x,y)$ be the probability of eventually exiting through a vertical side. Boundary values are $1$ on $x=0$ or $x=4$, and $0$ on $y=0$ or $y=4$. By symmetry, $P(2,2)=\frac12$.

Set

$$a=P(1,2),\qquad b=P(1,1)=P(1,3),\qquad c=P(2,1)=P(2,3).$$

The averaging property for a random walk gives

$$a=\frac{1+\frac12+2b}{4}=\frac38+\frac b2,$$

$$b=\frac{1+c+a}{4},\qquad c=\frac{2b+\frac12}{4}=\frac b2+\frac18.$$

Substitution gives $b=\frac12$ and therefore

$$a=\frac38+\frac14=\frac58.$$

## Knowledge points

- Random walks
- Harmonic averaging and symmetry

## Key idea

The probability at an interior lattice point is the average of the probabilities at its four neighbors.

## Common mistakes

- Assigning probability $1$ to every boundary point rather than only the vertical sides.
- Forgetting the symmetry value $P(2,2)=\frac12$.

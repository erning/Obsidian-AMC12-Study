# AMC12 2020-A Q21

![[assets/problems/2020-A/Q21.png]]

## Problem

How many positive integers $n$ are there such that $n$ is a multiple of $5$, and the least common multiple of $5!$ and $n$ equals $5$ times the greatest common divisor of $10!$ and $n$?

## Answer

**D.** $48$

## Solution

Write $n=2^a3^b5^c7^d$. No other prime can divide $n$, because its exponent would appear on the left but not on the right. Compare prime exponents in

$$\operatorname{lcm}(5!,n)=5\gcd(10!,n),$$

using

$$5!=2^3\cdot3\cdot5,\qquad10!=2^8\cdot3^4\cdot5^2\cdot7.$$

For the prime $2$,

$$\max(3,a)=1+\min(8,a),$$

so $a=3,4,5,6,7,8$: $6$ choices. For $3$,

$$\max(1,b)=1+\min(4,b),$$

so $b=1,2,3,4$: $4$ choices. For $5$, the equation forces $c=3$, and for $7$ it allows $d=0$ or $1$. Therefore the total is

$$6\cdot4\cdot1\cdot2=48.$$

## Knowledge points

- Prime-exponent comparison
- LCM and GCD
- Fundamental theorem of arithmetic

## Key idea

Translate the LCM/GCD identity into independent equations for each prime exponent.

## Common mistakes

- Forgetting that the outside factor $5$ changes only the exponent of prime $5$.
- Allowing primes not present in $10!$.

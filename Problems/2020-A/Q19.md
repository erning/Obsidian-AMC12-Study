# AMC12 2020-A Q19

![[assets/problems/2020-A/Q19.png]]

## Problem

There exists a unique strictly increasing sequence of nonnegative integers $a_1<a_2<\cdots<a_k$ such that

$$\frac{2^{289}+1}{2^{17}+1}=2^{a_1}+2^{a_2}+\cdots+2^{a_k}.$$

What is $k$?

## Answer

**C.** $137$

## Solution

Let $x=2^{17}$. Since $17$ is odd,

$$\frac{x^{17}+1}{x+1}=x^{16}-x^{15}+x^{14}-\cdots-x+1.$$

Pair each positive power with the negative power immediately below it:

$$x^{16}-x^{15}=2^{255}(2^{17}-1),$$

which is the sum of the $17$ powers $2^{255}$ through $2^{271}$. The same construction applies to

$$x^{14}-x^{13},\ x^{12}-x^{11},\ldots,\ x^2-x,$$

giving $8$ disjoint blocks of $17$ powers. The final $+1$ contributes one more power. Hence

$$k=8\cdot17+1=137.$$

## Knowledge points

- Geometric-series factorization
- Binary representation

## Key idea

Convert each difference $2^{17m+16}-2^{17m}$ into a block of $17$ consecutive binary powers.

## Common mistakes

- Assuming the alternating expression itself is a sum of positive powers.
- Counting only the $8$ blocks and forgetting the final $+1$.

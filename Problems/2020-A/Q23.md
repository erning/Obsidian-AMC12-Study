# AMC12 2020-A Q23

![[assets/problems/2020-A/Q23.png]]

## Problem

Jason rolls three fair standard six-sided dice. He then chooses a subset of the dice to reroll. After rerolling, he wins if and only if the sum of the three face-up numbers is exactly $7$. Jason optimizes his chances. What is the probability that he chooses to reroll exactly two dice?

## Answer

**A.** $\frac7{36}$

## Solution

Sort the original rolls as $u\le v\le w$. If two dice are rerolled and the die showing $u$ is kept, the winning probability is the number of ordered pairs summing to $7-u$, divided by $36$. For $u=1,2,3$, these probabilities are respectively $\frac5{36},\frac4{36},\frac3{36}$, all larger than rerolling all three dice, whose probability is $\frac{15}{216}=\frac5{72}$.

For rerolling exactly two to be strictly optimal, the two smallest original dice must satisfy $u+v\ge7$, so rerolling only one cannot reach $7$. We also need $u\le3$.

Count sorted triples:

- $u=1$: $1$ triple, $(1,6,6)$;
- $u=2$: $3$ triples;
- $u=3$: $6$ triples.

Accounting for permutations gives

$$3+12+27=42$$

ordered rolls. Thus the desired probability is

$$\frac{42}{6^3}=\frac{42}{216}=\frac7{36}.$$

## Knowledge points

- Conditional optimization
- Counting ordered dice outcomes
- Comparing expected winning probabilities

## Key idea

Compare the best strategy for each observed roll, then characterize exactly when keeping the smallest die is best.

## Common mistakes

- Counting only sorted triples and forgetting their permutations.
- Treating a tie between strategies as a unique choice without checking the strict optimum condition.

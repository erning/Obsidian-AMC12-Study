# AMC12 2022-B Q18

![[assets/problems/2022-B/Q18.png]]

## Problem

Each square in a $5\times5$ grid is filled or empty. A filled square remains filled if it has two or three filled neighbors, and an empty square becomes filled if it has exactly three filled neighbors. All other squares become empty. Suppose the grid has a border of empty squares surrounding a $3\times3$ subgrid. How many initial configurations lead to a transformed grid consisting of a single filled square in the center after one transformation? Rotations and reflections are considered different.

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(C) $22$**

## Solution

Only the inner $3\times3$ cells can initially be filled. For the center to be filled after the transformation:

- if the center starts empty, it must have exactly $3$ filled neighbors;
- if the center starts filled, it must have exactly $2$ or $3$ filled neighbors.

Every other inner cell must become empty, and every border cell must also remain empty. In particular, a configuration cannot place three filled cells along an outer edge of the $3\times3$ block, because the adjacent border cell would then be born.

The resulting enumeration can be organized as follows:

| Center initially | Filled neighbors of center | Valid patterns after excluding other births/survivals |
| --- | ---: | ---: |
| Empty | Exactly $3$ | $20$ |
| Filled | Exactly $2$ or $3$ | $2$ |

For the second row, the only valid patterns are the two diagonal arrangements
$$
\begin{matrix}1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&0&1\end{matrix}
\quad\text{and}\quad
\begin{matrix}0&0&1\\0&1&0\\1&0&0\end{matrix}.
$$
For an empty center, checking the $\binom83=56$ possible triples of filled neighbors and rejecting any pattern that produces another filled cell leaves $20$ patterns. Since rotations and reflections count separately,
$$
20+2=22.
$$

## Key idea

Impose the center condition first, then eliminate patterns that would create any other filled cell.

## Common mistakes

- Forgetting that diagonal cells are also neighbors.
- Identifying rotated configurations as the same when the problem counts them separately.

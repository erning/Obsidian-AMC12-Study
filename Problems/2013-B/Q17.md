# AMC12 2013-B Q17

![[assets/problems/2013-B/Q17.png]]

## Problem

Let $a$, $b$, and $c$ be real numbers such that
$$a+b+c=2,qquad a^2+b^2+c^2=12.$$
What is the difference between the maximum and minimum possible values of $c$?

## Answer

**D.** $\frac{16}{3}$

## Solution

For fixed $c$,
$$a+b=2-c,qquad a^2+b^2=12-c^2.$$
Real $a,b$ exist exactly when
$$
(a+b)^2\le2(a^2+b^2).
$$
Substituting gives
$$
(2-c)^2\le2(12-c^2).
$$
After simplification,
$$
(3c-10)(c+2)\le0,
$$
so
$$-2\le c\le\frac{10}{3}.$$
The requested difference is
$$\frac{10}{3}-(-2)=\frac{16}{3}.$$

## Knowledge points

- Quadratic inequalities
- Cauchy-Schwarz inequality
- Extremal values

## Key idea

The pair $(a,b)$ exists precisely when its sum and sum of squares satisfy the two-variable feasibility inequality.

## Common mistakes

- Assuming $a=b$ without checking both endpoints
- Reversing the inequality after expansion

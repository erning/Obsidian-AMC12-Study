# AMC12 2013-B Q16

![[assets/problems/2013-B/Q16.png]]

## Problem

Let $ABCDE$ be an equiangular convex pentagon of perimeter $1$. The pairwise intersections of the lines extending its sides determine a five-pointed star polygon. If $s$ is the perimeter of the star, what is the difference between the maximum and minimum possible values of $s$?

## Answer

**A.** $0$

## Solution

Every interior angle of an equiangular pentagon is $108^\circ$. Each point of the star therefore forms an isosceles triangle whose base angles are $72^\circ$ and whose apex angle is $36^\circ$.

By the sine rule, the two star sides attached to a given pentagon side are a fixed constant multiple $c$ of that side. Summing over all five vertices, the star perimeter is
$$s=2c(\text{pentagon perimeter})=2c.$$
It is independent of how the unit perimeter is distributed among the five sides. Hence its maximum and minimum are equal, and the difference is
$$0.$$

## Knowledge points

- Equiangular polygons
- Isosceles triangles
- Invariants

## Key idea

Each pentagon side contributes the same fixed multiple to the star perimeter, so only the total perimeter matters.

## Common mistakes

- Assuming an equiangular pentagon must be regular
- Optimizing individual side lengths before checking the invariant

# AMC12 2013-B Q18

![[assets/problems/2013-B/Q18.png]]

## Problem

Barbara and Jenna play a game with coins. Barbara removes $2$ or $4$ coins on her turn, except that if only one coin remains she loses her turn. Jenna removes $1$ or $3$ coins. Whoever removes the last coin wins. A coin flip determines who goes first. Who wins when the game starts with $2013$ coins and when it starts with $2014$ coins?

## Answer

**B.** Jenna wins with $2013$ coins; the first player wins with $2014$ coins.

## Solution

Work modulo $5$. Since $2013\equiv3\pmod5$, Jenna can win regardless of who starts. If she starts, she removes $3$ and leaves a multiple of $5$. Thereafter she responds to Barbara's removal of $2$ with $3$, or of $4$ with $1$, restoring a multiple of $5$. If Barbara starts, Jenna uses the same strategy to preserve the residue $3$ until Barbara is forced to leave one coin for Jenna.

For $2014\equiv4\pmod5$, the first player wins. Jenna can remove $1$ to reach the $2013$ case, while Barbara can remove $4$ and then preserve multiples of $5$ after each response. Thus the answer is choice B.

## Knowledge points

- Combinatorial games
- Modular invariants
- Strategy stealing by responses

## Key idea

Identify a residue class that lets a player answer every legal move with a complementary move.

## Common mistakes

- Treating Barbara's “one coin remains” rule as an ordinary move
- Pairing moves to preserve the wrong residue

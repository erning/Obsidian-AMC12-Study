# AMC12 2013-B Q23

![[assets/problems/2013-B/Q23.png]]

## Problem

Bernardo chooses a three-digit positive integer $N$ and writes its base-$5$ and base-$6$ representations. LeRoy treats the two written strings as base-$10$ integers and adds them to obtain $S$. For how many choices of $N$ are the two rightmost digits of $S$ the same, in order, as those of $2N$?

## Answer

**E.** $25$

## Solution

The last two base-$5$ digits determine $N\pmod{25}$, and the last two base-$6$ digits determine $N\pmod{36}$. Since
$$\operatorname{lcm}(25,36,100)=900,$$
the condition depends only on $N\pmod{900}$. It is enough to examine $0\le N<900$.

Let the last two base-$5$ digits be $a_1a_0$ and the last two base-$6$ digits be $b_1b_0$. Matching the units digits first forces
$$a_0=b_0.$$
Checking the tens digit modulo $100$ then leaves exactly five pairs:
$$
(a_1,b_1)=(0,0),(2,0),(4,0),(1,5),(3,5).
$$
There are $5$ choices for the common units digit $a_0=b_0$, so there are
$$5\cdot5=25$$
valid values of $N$.

## Knowledge points

- Number bases
- Modular arithmetic

## Key idea

Only the last two digits matter, so reduce the problem to simultaneous congruences modulo $25$, $36$, and $100$.

## Common mistakes

- Reading the base-$5$ or base-$6$ strings in their original bases
- Counting all three-digit numbers instead of residue classes modulo $900$

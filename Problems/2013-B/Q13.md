# AMC12 2013-B Q13

![[assets/problems/2013-B/Q13.png]]

## Problem

The internal angles of quadrilateral $ABCD$ form an arithmetic progression. Triangles $ABD$ and $DCB$ are similar with $\angle DBA=\angle DCB$ and $\angle ADB=\angle CBD$. Moreover, the angles in each of these two triangles also form an arithmetic progression. What is the largest possible sum of the two largest angles of $ABCD$?

## Answer

**D.** $240^\circ$

## Solution

Let
$$\angle DBA=x,\qquad\angle ADB=y.$$
The corresponding angles of the two similar triangles show that the four angles of the quadrilateral are
$$x,\ y,\ 180^\circ-y,\ 180^\circ-x.$$

The angles of a triangle in arithmetic progression have middle angle $60^\circ$. Combining this fact with the arithmetic-progression condition on the quadrilateral yields the two possible angle sets
$$
(60^\circ,80^\circ,100^\circ,120^\circ)
$$
and
$$
(45^\circ,75^\circ,105^\circ,135^\circ).
$$
The largest two angles in the second case sum to
$$105^\circ+135^\circ=240^\circ,$$
which is maximal.

## Knowledge points

- Arithmetic progressions of angles
- Similar triangles
- Casework

## Key idea

Use the $60^\circ$ middle angle condition in each triangle to restrict the quadrilateral's arithmetic progression.

## Common mistakes

- Assuming every arithmetic progression of four angles is possible
- Forgetting that the two triangle angle sets are linked by similarity

# AMC12 2013-B Q12

![[assets/problems/2013-B/Q12.png]]

## Problem

Cities $A,B,C,D,E$ are connected by roads $AB$, $AD$, $AE$, $BC$, $BD$, $CD$, and $DE$. How many different routes are there from $A$ to $B$ that use each road exactly once?

## Answer

**D.** $16$

## Solution

The two roads through $E$ form a detour from $A$ to $D$, and the two roads through $C$ form a detour from $B$ to $D$. Replace each detour by a parallel connection. The resulting multigraph has two $A$-$D$ connections, two $B$-$D$ connections, and the direct road $AB$.

The possible orders of these five connections in an $A$-to-$B$ trail are represented by four types:
$$ABDADB,\quad ADABDB,\quad ADBADB,\quad ADBDAB.$$
For each type, the $A$-$D$ detour can be assigned to either of its two passages, and independently the $B$-$D$ detour can be assigned to either passage. Thus each type gives $2\cdot2=4$ routes, for a total of
$$4\cdot4=16.$$

## Knowledge points

- Euler trails
- Graph compression
- Multiplication principle

## Key idea

Compress the two forced two-edge detours before counting the possible trail patterns.

## Common mistakes

- Treating the two detours as single labeled edges without restoring their choices
- Counting routes that do not use every road

# AMC12 2011-A Q25

![[assets/problems/2011-A/Q25.png]]

## Problem

Triangle $ABC$ has $\angle BAC = 60^\circ,$ $\angle CBA \le 90^\circ,$ $BC = 1,$ and $AC \ge AB.$ Let $H,$ $I,$ and $O$ be the orthocenter, incenter, and circumcenter of $\triangle ABC,$ respectively. Assume that the area of the pentagon $BCOIH$ is the maximum possible. What is $\angle CBA?$

(A) $60^\circ$
(B) $72^\circ$
(C) $75^\circ$
(D) $80^\circ$
(E) $90^\circ$

## Answer

**D.** $80^\circ$

## Solution

When $\angle BAC = 60^\circ,$ a classical fact is that $B,$ $C,$ $O,$ $I,$ and $H$ all lie on a common circle, so $BCOIH$ is a convex cyclic pentagon whose vertices depend only on the shape of the triangle.

Fixing $BC = 1$ and $\angle A = 60^\circ,$ the circumradius is $R = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt3},$ and $O, I, H$ are determined by $\angle CBA = B$ (with $\angle BCA = 120^\circ - B$). Writing the pentagon area as a function of $B$ on the allowed range $60^\circ \le B \le 90^\circ$ and maximizing gives an interior maximum at $B = 80^\circ.$

So the maximizing angle is $\angle CBA = 80^\circ.$

Thus, the correct answer is **D**.

## Knowledge points

- Circumcircle, circumcenter, and circumradius
- Optimization
- Trigonometry

## Key idea

Use the fixed $60^\circ$ angle and the circumradius to express the side lengths in terms of one angle, then maximize the circumradius constraint.

## Common mistakes

- Applying a formula before identifying the relevant quantities and constraints.
- Counting invalid cases or overlooking a boundary condition from the statement.

# AMC12 2011-A Q18

![[assets/problems/2011-A/Q18.png]]

## Problem

Suppose that $|x + y| + |x - y| = 2.$ What is the maximum possible value of $x^2 - 6x + y^2?$

(A) $5$
(B) $6$
(C) $7$
(D) $8$
(E) $9$

## Answer

**D.** $8$

## Solution

The identity $|x+y| + |x-y|$ $= 2\max(|x|, |y|)$ turns the condition into $\max(|x|, |y|) = 1,$ the boundary of the square with $|x| \le 1$ and $|y| \le 1.$

On this region $x^2 - 6x + y^2$ increases as $x$ decreases and as $y^2$ increases, so the maximum is at $x = -1,$ $y = \pm 1:$ $$ 1 + 6 + 1 = 8. $$

Thus, the correct answer is **D**.

## Key idea

Use symmetry to rewrite $|x+y|+|x-y|$ as $2\max(|x|,|y|)$, then maximize the square’s area under the resulting bound.

## Common mistakes

- Applying a formula before identifying the relevant quantities and constraints.
- Counting invalid cases or overlooking a boundary condition from the statement.

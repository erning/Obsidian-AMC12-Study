# AMC12 2011-A Q22

![[assets/problems/2011-A/Q22.png]]

## Problem

Let $R$ be a square region and $n \ge 4$ an integer. A point $X$ in the interior of $R$ is called $n$-ray partitional if there are $n$ rays emanating from $X$ that divide $R$ into $n$ triangles of equal area. How many points are $100$-ray partitional but not $60$-ray partitional?

(A) $1500$
(B) $1560$
(C) $2320$
(D) $2480$
(E) $2500$

## Answer

**C.** $2320$

## Solution

For even $n = 2m,$ the $n$-ray partitional points are exactly $\left(\tfrac{i}{m}, \tfrac{j}{m}\right)$ with $1 \le i, j \le m - 1,$ giving $(m-1)^2$ points.

For $n = 100$ ($m = 50$) there are $49^2 = 2401$ points. A point is both $100$- and $60$-ray partitional iff its coordinates are multiples of $\tfrac{1}{10},$ i.e. it is $20$-ray partitional, giving $9^2 = 81$ points.

So the count is $2401 - 81 = 2320.$

Thus, the correct answer is **C**.

## Key idea

The $2m$-ray partitional points form an $(m-1) \times (m-1)$ grid; subtract the points shared by the $100$- and $60$-ray grids (the $20$-ray grid).

- Applying a formula before identifying the relevant quantities and constraints.
- Counting invalid cases or overlooking a boundary condition from the statement.

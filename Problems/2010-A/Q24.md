# AMC12 2010-A Q24

![[assets/problems/2010-A/Q24.png]]

## Problem

Let $f(x)=\log_{10}\big(\sin(\pi x)\cdot\sin(2\pi x)\cdot\sin(3\pi x)\cdots\sin(8\pi x)\big).$ The intersection of the domain of $f(x)$ with the interval $[0,1]$ is a union of $n$ disjoint open intervals. What is $n?$

(A) $2$
(B) $12$
(C) $18$
(D) $22$
(E) $36$

## Answer

**B.** $12$

## Solution

Let $g(x)=\prod_{k=1}^8\sin(k\pi x);$ the domain of $f$ is where $g(x)\gt0.$ Since $\sin(k\pi(1-x))$ $=(-1)^{k+1}\sin(k\pi x)$ and $\sum_{k=1}^8(k+1)$ is even, $g(1-x)=g(x),$ so it suffices to study $\big(0,\tfrac12\big)$ and double.

In $\big(0,\tfrac12\big)$ the zeros of $g$ are the fractions $\tfrac{k}{n}$ with $2\le n\le8,$ $1\le k\lt\tfrac n2,$ and $\gcd(k,n)=1.$ For $n=2,\ldots,8$ there are $0,1,1,2,1,3,2$ of them, totaling $10.$

These $10$ zeros split $\big(0,\tfrac12\big)$ into $11$ subintervals on which $g$ has constant sign. Near $0$ every factor is positive, so $g\gt0$ there, and the sign flips at each zero except $\tfrac14=\tfrac28$ and $\tfrac13=\tfrac26,$ where an even number of factors vanish.

Tracking the signs, exactly $6$ of the $11$ subintervals have $g\gt0.$ By symmetry there are $6$ more in $\big(\tfrac12,1\big),$ so $n=12.$

Thus, **B** is the correct answer.

## Key idea

Use the product-to-sum identity to rewrite the logarithm as a sum of logarithms, then count the intervals where the sign changes.

## Common mistakes

- Applying a formula before identifying the relevant quantities and constraints.
- Counting invalid cases or overlooking a boundary condition from the statement.

# AMC12 2023-B Q25

![[assets/problems/2023-B/Q25.png]]

## Problem

A regular pentagon with area $\sqrt5+1$ is printed on paper and cut out. The five vertices of the pentagon are folded into the center of the pentagon, creating a smaller pentagon. What is the area of the new pentagon?

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(B) $\sqrt5-1$**

## Solution

![[assets/problems/2023-B/Q25-draft.svg]]

Let the original pentagon have circumradius $R$. Folding a vertex to the center creates a crease along the perpendicular bisector of the segment from the center to that vertex. Therefore each crease is at distance $\frac R2$ from the center, and the five creases form the new regular pentagon.

The original pentagon has apothem $R\cos36^\circ$, while the new one has apothem $\frac R2$. Since the area of similar regular pentagons is proportional to the square of the apothem,
$$
\frac{A_{\text{new}}}{A_{\text{original}}}
=\left(\frac{R/2}{R\cos36^\circ}\right)^2
=\frac1{4\cos^2 36^\circ}.
$$
Using
$$
\cos36^\circ=\frac{1+\sqrt5}{4},
$$
the ratio simplifies to
$$
\frac{3-\sqrt5}{2}.
$$
Multiplying by the original area gives
$$
A_{\text{new}}
=(\sqrt5+1)\frac{3-\sqrt5}{2}
=\boxed{\sqrt5-1}.
$$

## Key idea

Compare the apothems of the original and folded pentagons.

## Common mistakes

- Using the circumradius instead of the apothem when comparing areas.

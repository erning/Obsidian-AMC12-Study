# AMC12 2015-B Q23

![[assets/problems/2015-B/Q23.png]]

## Problem

A rectangular box measures $a\times b\times c$, where $a,b,c$ are integers and $1\le a\le b\le c$. The volume and surface area are numerically equal. How many ordered triples $(a,b,c)$ are possible?

## Answer

**B.** $10$

## Solution

Equality of volume and surface area gives

$$abc=2(ab+bc+ca).$$

For fixed $a$, rearranging and factoring yields a short list:

- $a=1$ and $a=2$ give no solutions.
- For $a=3$, $(b-6)(c-6)=36$, giving $5$ ordered triples with $b\le c$.
- For $a=4$, $(b-4)(c-4)=16$, giving $3$ triples.
- For $a=5$, $(3b-10)(3c-10)=100$, giving $1$ triple.
- For $a=6$, $(b-3)(c-3)=9$, giving $1$ triple.

Thus the total number is

$$5+3+1+1=10.$$

## Knowledge points

- Surface area and volume
- Factoring Diophantine equations
- Ordered constraints

## Key idea

Bound $a$ first, then factor the equation for each possible smallest dimension.

## Common mistakes

- Counting permutations separately despite $a\le b\le c$
- Forgetting the factor $2$ in the surface area
- Continuing to search after the bounded cases

# AMC12 2009-B Q24

![[assets/problems/2009-B/Q24.png]]

## Problem

For how many values of $x$ in $[0, \pi]$ is $\sin^{-1}(\sin 6x) = \cos^{-1}(\cos x)?$

Note: The functions $\sin^{-1} = \arcsin$ and $\cos^{-1} = \arccos$ denote inverse trigonometric functions.

(A) $3$
(B) $4$
(C) $5$
(D) $6$
(E) $7$

## Answer

**B.** $4$

## Solution

On $[0, \pi],\ \cos^{-1}(\cos x) = x.$ Since $\sin^{-1}$ takes values in $[-\tfrac{\pi}{2}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}],$ any solution requires $x \in [0, \tfrac{\pi}{2}],$ where the equation becomes $\sin 6x = \sin x.$

As $x$ goes from $0$ to $\tfrac{\pi}{2},$ $\sin 6x$ runs $0 \to 1 \to -1 \to 1 \to 0$ (peaks at $\tfrac{\pi}{12}, \tfrac{5\pi}{12},$ trough at $\tfrac{\pi}{4}$), while $\sin x$ increases from $0$ to $1.$

Besides $x = 0,$ the graphs cross once in each of $[\tfrac{\pi}{12}, \tfrac{\pi}{4}],$ $[\tfrac{\pi}{4}, \tfrac{5\pi}{12}],$ and $[\tfrac{5\pi}{12}, \tfrac{\pi}{2}],$ for $4$ solutions in all.

Thus, the correct answer is **B**.

## Key idea

Use inverse-trigonometric identities and count the intervals on which the principal-value equality is valid.

## Common mistakes

- Applying a formula before identifying the relevant quantities and constraints.
- Counting invalid cases or overlooking a boundary condition from the statement.

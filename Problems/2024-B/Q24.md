# AMC12 2024-B Q24

![[assets/problems/2024-B/Q24.png]]

## Problem

How many ordered triples $(a,b,c)$ of positive integers with $a\le b\le c\le9$ can be the three altitudes of a nondegenerate triangle with integer inradius?

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(B) $3$**

## Solution

Let the area be $K$ and the three altitudes be $a,b,c$. The corresponding side lengths are proportional to

$$
\frac1a,\qquad\frac1b,\qquad\frac1c,
$$

because each side times its altitude equals $2K$.

The semiperimeter is therefore proportional to

$$
\frac1a+\frac1b+\frac1c.
$$

Since $r=K/s$, the inradius condition becomes

$$
\frac1a+\frac1b+\frac1c=\frac1r
$$

for a positive integer $r$. We also need the reciprocal side lengths to satisfy the strict triangle inequality:

$$
\frac1a<\frac1b+\frac1c.
$$

Checking $1\le a\le b\le c\le9$ gives exactly

$$
(3,3,3),\qquad(6,6,6),\qquad(9,9,9).
$$

They correspond to equilateral triangles with integer inradii $1,2,3$. All other reciprocal-sum solutions are degenerate, such as $(2,3,6)$ and $(4,8,8)$. Hence the count is

$$
\boxed3.
$$

## Knowledge points

- Altitude-area relation
- Inradius formula
- Triangle inequality

## Key idea

Convert altitudes into reciprocal side lengths and impose the integer-inradius condition.

## Common mistakes

- Treating the altitudes themselves as side lengths.
- Allowing equality in the triangle inequality.

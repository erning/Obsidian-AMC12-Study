# AMC12 2024-B Q10

![[assets/problems/2024-B/Q10.png]]

## Problem

A list of $9$ real numbers consists of

$$
1,2.2,3.2,5.2,6.2,7
$$

and $x\le y\le z$. The range is $7$, and both the mean and median are positive integers. How many ordered triples $(x,y,z)$ are possible?

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(C) $3$**

## Solution

The fixed numbers sum to

$$
1+2.2+3.2+5.2+6.2+7=24.8.
$$

The mean is integral exactly when $x+y+z$ has fractional part $0.2$.

The fixed numbers already range from $1$ to $7$, so the new triple must extend the range to exactly $7$. This can happen in only three ways: the minimum drops to $0$ (so $x=0$), the maximum rises to $8$ (so $z=8$), or both ends move outward with $z-x=7$.

Combining this with an integer mean (so $x+y+z$ has fractional part $0.2$) and an integer median, and checking the position of $x,y,z$ relative to the fixed values, gives exactly:

$$
(0,5,6.2),\qquad(0.1,4,7.1),\qquad(6,6.2,8).
$$

Their means and medians are respectively $(4,5)$, $(4,4)$, and $(5,6)$, so all are valid. Hence the answer is

$$
\boxed3.
$$

## Knowledge points

- Mean and median
- Range constraints
- Finite casework

## Key idea

The fixed entries force the new values to extend the range by exactly one.

## Common mistakes

- Treating $x,y,z$ as unordered.
- Checking the mean but not the median.
- Allowing the range to exceed $7$.

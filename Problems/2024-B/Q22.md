# AMC12 2024-B Q22

![[assets/problems/2024-B/Q22.png]]

## Problem

Let $\triangle ABC$ have integer side lengths and satisfy $\angle B=2\angle A$. What is the least possible perimeter?

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(C) $15$**

## Solution

Let the side lengths opposite $A,B,C$ be $a,b,c$. Since $B=2A$, the Law of Sines and the double-angle identity imply

$$
\frac{b}{a}=\frac{\sin2A}{\sin A}=2\cos A.
$$

Using the Law of Cosines to eliminate $\cos A$ yields

$$
b^2=a(a+c).
$$

Therefore

$$
c=\frac{b^2}{a}-a.
$$

Search the smallest positive integers with $a\le b$ and integral positive $c$. For $a=1,2,3$, the smallest admissible $b$ is $b=2a$, which gives $c=3a=a+b$ — a degenerate triangle — while any larger $b$ makes $c$ too large for the triangle inequality. The first valid case is

$$
a=4,\qquad b=6,\qquad c=\frac{36}{4}-4=5.
$$

The sides $4,5,6$ form a nondegenerate triangle and satisfy $6^2=4(4+5)$. Its perimeter is

$$
4+5+6=\boxed{15}.
$$

- Law of Sines

## Key idea

The angle condition becomes the Diophantine relation $b^2=a(a+c)$.

## Common mistakes

- Assigning $a,b,c$ to the wrong opposite angles.
- Accepting a triple that fails the triangle inequality.

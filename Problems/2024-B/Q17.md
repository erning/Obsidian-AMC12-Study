# AMC12 2024-B Q17

![[assets/problems/2024-B/Q17.png]]

## Problem

Integers $a$ and $b$ are chosen without replacement from $\{-10,-9,\ldots,10\}$. What is the probability that

$$
x^3+ax^2+bx+6
$$

has three distinct integer roots?

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(C) $\frac1{105}$**

## Solution

There are $21\cdot20=420$ ordered choices of $(a,b)$.

If the roots are distinct integers $p,q,r$, then

$$
pqr=-6,\qquad a=-(p+q+r),\qquad b=pq+pr+qr.
$$

The unordered distinct root sets with product $-6$ are

$$
\{1,2,-3\},\ \{1,-2,3\},\ \{-1,2,3\},\ \{-1,-2,-3\},\ \{1,-1,6\}.
$$

They give the coefficient pairs

$$
(0,-7),\ (-2,-5),\ (-4,1),\ (6,11),\ (-6,-1).
$$

The pair $(6,11)$ is invalid because $b=11$ is outside the allowed range. The other four are valid and distinct. Therefore

$$
\Pr=\frac4{420}=\boxed{\frac1{105}}.
$$

## Knowledge points

- Vieta's formulas
- Integer factorization
- Ordered probability

## Key idea

Enumerate possible integer root triples using the constant term.

## Common mistakes

- Counting unordered $(a,b)$ even though the choices are ordered.
- Forgetting the bounds $|a|,|b|\le10$.

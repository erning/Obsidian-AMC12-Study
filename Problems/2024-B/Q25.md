# AMC12 2024-B Q25

![[assets/problems/2024-B/Q25.png]]

## Problem

Each of $6$ balls is independently assigned one of two colors, red or blue, and one of two patterns, striped or dotted. Frida selects one ball uniformly. The probability that the events “the selected ball is red” and “the selected ball is striped” are independent can be written as $\frac{m}{n}$ in lowest terms. What is $m$?

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(A) $243$**

## Solution

For a particular outcome, let:

- $R$ be the number of red balls;
- $S$ be the number of striped balls;
- $K$ be the number of red-striped balls.

For a uniformly selected ball,

$$
P(\text{red})=\frac{R}{6},\qquad
P(\text{striped})=\frac{S}{6},\qquad
P(\text{both})=\frac{K}{6}.
$$

Independence requires

$$
\frac K6=\frac R6\cdot\frac S6,
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
6K=RS.
$$

There are $4^6=4096$ equally likely color-pattern assignments. For fixed category counts, the number of assignments is

$$
\frac{6!}{K!(R-K)!(S-K)!(6-R-S+K)!}.
$$

Summing this over all nonnegative counts satisfying $6K=RS$ gives $972$ favorable assignments. Hence

$$
\Pr=\frac{972}{4096}=\frac{243}{1024}.
$$

The problem asks for the numerator $m$, so

$$
m=\boxed{243}.
$$

## Knowledge points

- Independence of events
- Multinomial counting
- Casework on category counts

## Key idea

Translate event independence into the count equation $6K=RS$.

## Common mistakes

- Assuming independence automatically because the ball is selected uniformly.
- Counting only color assignments and ignoring patterns.
- Returning the denominator when the question asks for the numerator.

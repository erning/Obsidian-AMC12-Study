# AMC12 2011-B Q11

![[assets/problems/2011-B/Q11.png]]

## Problem

A frog located at $(x, y),$ with both $x$ and $y$ integers, makes successive jumps of length $5$ and always lands on points with integer coordinates. Suppose that the frog starts at $(0, 0)$ and ends at $(1, 0).$ What is the smallest possible number of jumps the frog makes?

(A) $2$
(B) $3$
(C) $4$
(D) $5$
(E) $6$

## Answer

**B.** $3$

## Solution

One jump cannot work, since $(0,0)$ and $(1,0)$ are only $1$ apart. Two jumps also fail: the intermediate point would be at distance $5$ from both, forcing it onto the perpendicular bisector $x=\frac12,$ which contains no lattice points.

Three jumps suffice, for example $$ (0,0)\to(3,4)\to(6,0)\to(1,0), $$ where each step has length $5.$

Thus, the correct answer is **B**.

## Knowledge points

- Lattice point
- Distance formula

## Key idea

The shortest valid frog path is a lattice distance problem; test integer points in the bounded region and minimize the squared distance.

## Common mistakes

- Applying a formula before identifying the relevant quantities and constraints.
- Counting invalid cases or overlooking a boundary condition from the statement.

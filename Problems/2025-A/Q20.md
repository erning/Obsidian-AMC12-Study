# AMC12 2025-A Q20

![[assets/problems/2025-A/Q20.png]]

## Problem

The base of the pentahedron is a $13\times8$ rectangle. Its lateral faces are two isosceles triangles with base 8 and equal sides 13, and two isosceles trapezoids with bases 7 and 13 and nonparallel sides 13. What is its volume?

**Choices:** (A) 416 &nbsp;&nbsp; (B) 520 &nbsp;&nbsp; (C) 528 &nbsp;&nbsp; (D) 676 &nbsp;&nbsp; (E) 832

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(C) 528**

## Solution

The top edge is a ridge of length $7$, centered over the $13\times8$ base. Its endpoints are horizontally $3$ units from the two short-side corners and $4$ units from the two long-side corners.

Let $h$ be the height of the ridge. A slant edge of length 13 gives

$$
3^2+4^2+h^2=13^2,
$$

so $h=12$.

At height $z$, a horizontal cross-section is a rectangle. Its dimensions shrink linearly:

$$
13-\frac z2\quad\text{and}\quad 8-\frac{2z}{3}.
$$

Equivalently, at heights $0,6,12$, the cross-sectional areas are

$$
A_0=13\cdot8=104,\qquad
A_6=10\cdot4=40,\qquad
A_{12}=0.
$$

Apply the prismatoid formula:

$$
V=\frac h6(A_0+4A_6+A_{12})
=\frac{12}{6}(104+4\cdot40)
=\boxed{528}.
$$

## Knowledge points

- Cross-sections of polyhedra
- Linear scaling with height
- Prismatoid volume formula
- Three-dimensional Pythagorean theorem

## Key idea

Find the height from a $3$-$4$-$5$ triangle, then use the three horizontal cross-sections at heights $0,6,12$.

## Common mistakes

- Treating the solid as a prism with constant cross-section.
- Using the slant edge 13 as the height.
- Applying the pyramid formula instead of the prismatoid formula.

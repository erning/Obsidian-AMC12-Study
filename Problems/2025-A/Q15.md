# AMC12 2025-A Q15

![[assets/problems/2025-A/Q15.png]]

## Problem

A set is sum-free if whenever $x$ and $y$ are not necessarily distinct elements of the set, $x+y$ is not an element of the set. What is the greatest possible size of a sum-free subset of $\{1,2,\ldots,20\}$?

**Choices:** (A) 8 &nbsp;&nbsp; (B) 9 &nbsp;&nbsp; (C) 10 &nbsp;&nbsp; (D) 11 &nbsp;&nbsp; (E) 12

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(C) 10**

## Solution

The set

$$
\{11,12,\ldots,20\}
$$

is sum-free: the sum of any two elements is at least $22$, outside $\{1,\ldots,20\}$. Thus the maximum is at least 10.

For an upper bound, let

$$
S=\{a_1<a_2<\cdots<a_k\}
$$

be sum-free, with largest element $a_k$. For each $i<k$, the difference $a_k-a_i$ cannot be in $S$, because otherwise

$$
(a_k-a_i)+a_i=a_k
$$

would violate sum-freeness.

These $k-1$ differences are distinct and are disjoint from the $k$ elements of $S$, all lying in $\{1,\ldots,20\}$. Hence

$$
k+(k-1)\le20,
$$

so $k\le10$. Therefore the maximum is

$$
\boxed{10}.
$$

## Knowledge points

- Extremal combinatorics
- Sum-free sets
- Constructing a lower bound
- Proving an upper bound

## Key idea

Give a 10-element construction, then show every $k$-element sum-free set forces $k-1$ additional excluded differences.

## Common mistakes

- Checking only distinct $x,y$; the definition allows $x=y$.
- Giving a large example without proving that no larger set exists.
- Assuming the largest possible set must be the top half without establishing the upper bound.

# AMC12 2025-A Q25

![[assets/problems/2025-A/Q25.png]]

## Problem

Polynomials $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$ each have degree 3 and leading coefficient 1, and their roots are all elements of $\{1,2,3,4,5\}$. The function

$$
f(x)=\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}
$$

has the property that there exist real numbers $a<b<c<d$ such that the set of all real $x$ for which $f(x)\le0$ is

$$
[a,b]\cup(c,d).
$$

How many functions $f(x)$ are possible?

**Choices:** (A) 7 &nbsp;&nbsp; (B) 9 &nbsp;&nbsp; (C) 11 &nbsp;&nbsp; (D) 12 &nbsp;&nbsp; (E) 13

## Answer

The wording asks for distinct functions $f$, but the listed answer **(E) 13** is
the count of admissible ordered pairs $(P,Q)$. Under the literal wording, the
number of distinct functions is **8**, which is not among the choices.

The problem was therefore flawed as printed. For the intended interpretation,
record **(E) 13**; for the literal interpretation, record **8**.

## Solution

The sign of $f(x)$ can change only at $1,2,3,4,5$, and $f(x)>0$ for sufficiently large positive and negative $x$, because $P$ and $Q$ have the same degree and positive leading coefficient.

For the set $\{f\le0\}=[a,b]\cup(c,d)$:

- $a$ and $b$ must be zeros of $f$, so the numerator must dominate the denominator at those roots; these endpoints are included.
- $c$ and $d$ must be poles of $f$, so the denominator must dominate the numerator at those roots; these endpoints are excluded.
- The sign pattern across the five allowed root locations must be positive, negative, positive, negative, positive, with the endpoint inclusion/exclusion determined by zero versus pole.

Now enumerate. $P$ already has two simple roots $a,b$ (the zeros) and needs one more root; $Q$ already has two simple roots $c,d$ (the poles) and needs one more root. These two ÔÇťextraÔÇŁ roots must coincide at some point $t$; otherwise $f$ would gain an odd-multiplicity zero or pole at the fifth location and break the sign pattern. Thus $t\in\{1,2,3,4,5\}$, with $P$ and $Q$ sharing a simple root at $t$ that cancels on reduction (so $f$ does not change sign at $t$).

$t$ cannot be $a$ or $b$: that would make an endpoint a common root of $P$ and $Q$, so $f$ would be undefined there, contradicting $a,b\in\{f\le0\}$. The value $t$ may be $c$ or $d$ (poles are already excluded from $\{f\le0\}$), or the fifth point $s$ (the element not in $\{a,b,c,d\}$), provided $s$ does not fall inside one of the negative intervals $(a,b)$ or $(c,d)$, which would punch a hole in it.

Classifying by the omitted point $s\in\{1,2,3,4,5\}$ (with $a,b,c,d$ the remaining four in increasing order), the admissible $t$ values are:

| $s$ | $a,b,c,d$ | location of $s$ | admissible $t$ | $(P,Q)$ pairs |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $1$ | $2,3,4,5$ | $(-\infty,a)$ | $s,c,d$ | $3$ |
| $2$ | $1,3,4,5$ | $(a,b)$ | $c,d$ | $2$ |
| $3$ | $1,2,4,5$ | $(b,c)$ | $s,c,d$ | $3$ |
| $4$ | $1,2,3,5$ | $(c,d)$ | $c,d$ | $2$ |
| $5$ | $1,2,3,4$ | $(d,\infty)$ | $s,c,d$ | $3$ |

So the number of ordered polynomial pairs $(P,Q)$ is

$$
3+2+3+2+3=\mathbf{13}.
$$

If instead we count distinct functions $f$: the reduced expression is determined by $s$ (giving $5$ functions), but when $t=s$ the function is undefined at $s$, so its domain is smaller than when $t=c$ or $t=d$. Thus the cases $s=1,3,5$ each contribute one additional function with a different domain. The number of distinct functions is therefore

$$
5+3=\mathbf{8},
$$

which is not among the choices.

The question asks for ÔÇťhow many functions $f(x)$,ÔÇŁ whose literal answer is $8$; the published answer **(E) 13** corresponds to ordered polynomial pairs $(P,Q)$.

- Root multiplicity

## Key idea

Zeros and poles control both the sign changes and whether the interval endpoints are included.

## Common mistakes

- Treating every root of $P$ or $Q$ as a sign change.
- Ignoring common roots and removable discontinuities.
- Counting pairs $(P,Q)$ instead of distinct functions without checking whether common factors cancel.

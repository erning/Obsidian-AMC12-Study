# AMC12 2025-A Q25

![[assets/problems/2025-A/Q25.png]]

## Problem

Polynomials $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$ each have degree 3 and leading coefficient 1, and their roots are all elements of $\{1,2,3,4,5\}$. The function

$$
f(x)=\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}
$$

has the property that there exist real numbers $a<b<c<d$ such that the set of all real $x$ for which $f(x)\le0$ is

$$
[a,b]\cup(c,d).
$$

How many functions $f(x)$ are possible?

**Choices:** (A) 7 &nbsp;&nbsp; (B) 9 &nbsp;&nbsp; (C) 11 &nbsp;&nbsp; (D) 12 &nbsp;&nbsp; (E) 13

## Answer

- Official answer: **(E) 13**
- Verification status: **Needs review**

## Solution

The sign of $f(x)$ can change only at $1,2,3,4,5$, and $f(x)>0$ for sufficiently large positive and negative $x$, because $P$ and $Q$ have the same degree and positive leading coefficient.

For the set $\{f\le0\}=[a,b]\cup(c,d)$:

- $a$ and $b$ must be zeros of $f$, so the numerator must dominate the denominator at those roots; these endpoints are included.
- $c$ and $d$ must be poles of $f$, so the denominator must dominate the numerator at those roots; these endpoints are excluded.
- The sign pattern across the five allowed root locations must be positive, negative, positive, negative, positive, with the endpoint inclusion/exclusion determined by zero versus pole.

A complete solution requires enumerating the possible root multiplicities of $P$ and $Q$, including common roots and cancellation. The source archive itself notes that the problem is flawed: the original wording asked for the number of functions, but the published answer does not agree cleanly with an independent count of distinct rational functions. Therefore the official key value 13 is retained here, but this problem should not be used as a verified example until the statement and intended counting convention are clarified.

## Key idea

Zeros and poles control both the sign changes and whether the interval endpoints are included.

## Common mistakes

- Treating every root of $P$ or $Q$ as a sign change.
- Ignoring common roots and removable discontinuities.
- Counting pairs $(P,Q)$ instead of distinct functions without checking whether common factors cancel.

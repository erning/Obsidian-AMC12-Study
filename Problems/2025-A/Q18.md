# AMC12 2025-A Q18

![[assets/problems/2025-A/Q18.png]]

## Problem

How many ordered triples $(x,y,z)$ of distinct nonnegative integers at most 8 satisfy

$$
xy>z,\qquad zx>y,\qquad yz>x?
$$

**Choices:** (A) 36 &nbsp;&nbsp; (B) 84 &nbsp;&nbsp; (C) 186 &nbsp;&nbsp; (D) 336 &nbsp;&nbsp; (E) 486

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(C) 186**

## Solution

No variable can be zero. For example, if $z=0$, then $zx=0$, which cannot be greater than the positive number $y$. Thus $x,y,z$ are distinct elements of $\{1,\ldots,8\}$.

Take three values $a<b<c$. Among the three products, the only potentially restrictive inequality is

$$
ab>c.
$$

Indeed, $ac>b$ and $bc>a$ automatically because $a,b,c$ are positive and distinct. The conditions are symmetric, so every valid 3-element set produces all $3!=6$ ordered triples.

Count the 3-element subsets of $\{1,\ldots,8\}$ satisfying $ab>c$, tabulated by the largest element $c$ (with $a<b<c$):

| $c$ | 3 | 4 | 5 | 6 | 7 | 8 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| number of $\{a,b\}$ with $ab>c$ | 0 | 1 | 3 | 5 | 9 | 13 |

The total is $0+1+3+5+9+13=31$ subsets. (Equivalently, $\binom83=56$ and $25$ subsets fail $ab>c$.) Therefore,

$$
6(31)=\boxed{186}.
$$

## Knowledge points

- Symmetry in inequalities
- Reducing ordered counting to subset counting
- Permutations

## Key idea

Sort the three values first. The smallest two determine the only nonautomatic inequality; restore the six orders at the end.

## Common mistakes

- Counting ordered triples directly and overcounting.
- Forgetting to exclude zero.
- Assuming all three inequalities are equally difficult after ordering.

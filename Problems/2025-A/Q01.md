# AMC12 2025-A Q01

![[assets/problems/2025-A/Q01.png]]

## Problem

Andy and Betsy both live in Mathville. Andy leaves Mathville on his bicycle at 1:30, traveling due north at a steady 8 miles per hour. Betsy leaves on her bicycle from the same point at 2:30, traveling due east at a steady 12 miles per hour. At what time will they be exactly the same distance from their common starting point?

**Choices:** (A) 3:30 &nbsp;&nbsp; (B) 3:45 &nbsp;&nbsp; (C) 4:00 &nbsp;&nbsp; (D) 4:15 &nbsp;&nbsp; (E) 4:30

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(E) 4:30**

## Solution

### Method 1: Compare distances at 2:30

At 2:30, Andy has already ridden for one hour, so he is

$$
8 \times 1=8
$$

miles from Mathville.

After 2:30, let $t$ be the number of hours that have passed.

- Andy's distance from Mathville is $8+8t$ miles.
- Betsy's distance from Mathville is $12t$ miles.

They are the same distance from Mathville when

$$
8+8t=12t.
$$

Therefore,

$$
8=4t
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
t=2.
$$

Two hours after 2:30 is 4:30. Thus the answer is

$$
\boxed{\text{(E) 4:30}}.
$$

### Method 2: Head start and relative speed

At 2:30, Andy has an 8-mile head start. From then on, Betsy travels 4 miles per hour faster than Andy:

$$
12-8=4.
$$

It takes Betsy

$$
\frac{8}{4}=2
$$

hours to make up the 8-mile difference. Therefore, they are equally far from Mathville at 4:30.

## Knowledge points

- Uniform motion: $\text{distance}=\text{speed}\times\text{time}$
- Translating a time delay into an initial distance difference
- Setting two linear distance expressions equal
- Relative speed

## Key idea

Use 2:30 as the reference time. Andy's one-hour head start gives him an 8-mile lead, while Betsy gains 4 miles per hour, so the lead takes 2 hours to disappear.

## Common mistakes

- Comparing Andy's and Betsy's total travel times instead of their distances from Mathville.
- Forgetting that Andy has already traveled for one hour when Betsy starts.
- Assuming that because they travel north and east, their distances from each other must be compared. The question asks for each person's distance from the common starting point, so the directions do not affect the equation.

# AMC12 2025-A Q06

![[assets/problems/2025-A/Q06.png]]

## Problem

Six chairs are arranged around a round table. Two students and two teachers randomly select four of the chairs to sit in. What is the probability that the two students will sit in two adjacent chairs and the two teachers will also sit in two adjacent chairs?

**Choices:** (A) $\frac16$ &nbsp;&nbsp; (B) $\frac15$ &nbsp;&nbsp; (C) $\frac29$ &nbsp;&nbsp; (D) $\frac3{13}$ &nbsp;&nbsp; (E) $\frac14$

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(B) $\frac15$**

## Solution

Treat the students and teachers as two distinct pairs. The total number of assignments is

$$
\binom62\binom42=15\cdot6=90.
$$

Choose the two chairs occupied by the students first. There are 6 adjacent pairs around the table. Once the students occupy one such pair, the remaining four chairs form a path of four chairs. Among those four chairs, exactly 3 pairs are adjacent on the original table, so there are 3 choices for the teachers.

Thus the number of favorable assignments is

$$
6\cdot3=18.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\Pr(\text{desired})=\frac{18}{90}=\boxed{\frac15}.
$$

## Knowledge points

- Counting combinations
- Circular adjacency
- Favourable outcomes divided by total outcomes

## Key idea

There are six adjacent pairs for the students. After fixing one student pair, the remaining chairs are no longer a circle; they form a four-chair path with exactly three adjacent pairs.

## Common mistakes

- Treating the four remaining chairs as a circle and counting 4 adjacent pairs.
- Counting seat arrangements of individual students and teachers when only the occupied chair sets matter.
- Forgetting that the students and teachers occupy disjoint chairs.

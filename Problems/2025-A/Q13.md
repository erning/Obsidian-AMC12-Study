# AMC12 2025-A Q13

![[assets/problems/2025-A/Q13.png]]

## Problem

Let $C=\{1,2,3,\ldots,13\}$. Let $N$ be the greatest integer such that there exists an $N$-element subset of $C$ containing no five consecutive integers. If $N$ integers are chosen at random from $C$, what is the probability that the chosen set contains no five consecutive integers?

**Choices:** (A) $\frac3{130}$ &nbsp;&nbsp; (B) $\frac3{143}$ &nbsp;&nbsp; (C) $\frac5{143}$ &nbsp;&nbsp; (D) $\frac1{26}$ &nbsp;&nbsp; (E) $\frac5{78}$

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(D) $\frac1{26}$**

## Solution

To avoid five consecutive numbers, two removed elements are enough; for example, removing 5 and 10 breaks every block of five. One removed element cannot break all nine possible blocks, so

$$
N=11.
$$

Choosing 11 elements from 13 is equivalent to removing 2 elements. There are

$$
\binom{13}{2}=78
$$

possible removed pairs.

The nine blocks of five are

$$
\{1,\ldots,5\},\{2,\ldots,6\},\ldots,\{9,\ldots,13\}.
$$

For every block to be hit by the two removed elements, one removed element must be near the beginning and one near the end. Checking the boundary possibilities gives exactly

$$
\{4,9\},\quad \{5,9\},\quad \{5,10\}.
$$

Thus the probability is

$$
\frac3{78}=\boxed{\frac1{26}}.
$$

## Key idea

Because $N=11$, count the two omitted numbers rather than the eleven selected numbers. The condition becomes a hitting problem for the nine length-5 intervals.

## Common mistakes

- Counting all subsets of all sizes instead of first finding $N$.
- Forgetting that the chosen set avoids a block exactly when the omitted pair hits that block.
- Counting ordered pairs instead of unordered pairs.

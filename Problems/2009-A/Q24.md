# AMC12 2009-A Q24

![[assets/problems/2009-A/Q24.png]]

## Problem

The tower function of twos is defined recursively as follows: $T(1) = 2$ and $T(n + 1) = 2^{T(n)}$ for $n \ge 1.$ Let $A = (T(2009))^{T(2009)}$ and $B = (T(2009))^A.$ What is the largest integer $k$ such that $$\underbrace{\log_2 \log_2 \log_2 \ldots \log_2}_{k} B$$ is defined?

(A) $2009$
(B) $2010$
(C) $2011$
(D) $2012$
(E) $2013$

## Answer

**E.** $2013$

## Solution

Since $\log_2 T(n + 1) = T(n),$ each application of $\log_2$ strips one $2$ off the top of a tower of twos.

Reducing $B = (T(2009))^A$ with $A = (T(2009))^{T(2009)},$ one finds $\log_2 B = A\cdot T(2008),$ $\log_2^2 B = T(2009)T(2008)$ $+ T(2007),$ and in general the dominant term after $k + 3$ logs is $T(2008 - k).$

So after $2012$ applications of $\log_2$ the result is still positive, meaning a $2013$th $\log_2$ is defined. A matching upper bound shows the result becomes negative after $2013$ applications, so a $2014$th $\log_2$ is undefined. Hence the largest $k$ is $2013.$

Thus, the correct answer is **E**.

## Knowledge points

- Recursion
- Logarithm
- Exponent

## Key idea

Each $\log_2$ peels one layer off a tower of twos; track the dominant term of the reduced expressions to find when the iterated logarithm first goes negative.

## Common mistakes

- Applying a formula before identifying the relevant quantities and constraints.
- Counting invalid cases or overlooking a boundary condition from the statement.

# AMC12 2014-B Q13

![[assets/problems/2014-B/Q13.png]]

## Problem

Real numbers $a,b$ satisfy $1<a<b$, and no positive-area triangle has side lengths $1,a,b$ or $\frac1a,\frac1b,1$. What is the smallest possible value of $b$?

## Answer

**C.** $\frac{3+\sqrt5}{2}$

## Solution

For $1,a,b$ not to form a triangle, the largest side must satisfy
$$b\ge a+1.$$
For $\frac1a,\frac1b,1$ not to form a triangle,
$$\frac1a+\frac1b\le1,$$
or
$$a\ge\frac{b}{b-1}.$$
At the minimum the bounds meet, so set $a=b-1$:
$$b-1=\frac{b}{b-1}.$$
This gives
$$b^2-3b+1=0,$$
and the larger root is
$$b=\frac{3+\sqrt5}{2}.$$

## Knowledge points

- Triangle inequality
- Optimization
- Quadratic equations

## Key idea

Make both non-triangle conditions tight at the minimum.

## Common mistakes

- Using $a+b\le 1$ in the wrong triangle
- Choosing the smaller quadratic root

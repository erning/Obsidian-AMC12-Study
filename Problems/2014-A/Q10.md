# AMC12 2014-A Q10

![[assets/problems/2014-A/Q10.png]]

## Problem

Three congruent isosceles triangles have their bases on the sides of an equilateral triangle of side length $1$. The sum of their areas equals the area of the equilateral triangle. What is the length of one of the two congruent sides of one isosceles triangle?

## Answer

**B.** $\frac{\sqrt3}{3}$

## Solution

The equilateral triangle has area $\sqrt3/4$, so each small triangle has area $\sqrt3/12$. If $h$ is the height of one small triangle, then
$$\frac12(1)h=\frac{\sqrt3}{12},\qquad h=\frac{\sqrt3}{6}.$$
Its congruent side has length
$$\sqrt{\left(\frac12\right)^2+\left(\frac{\sqrt3}{6}\right)^2}
=\sqrt{\frac14+\frac1{12}}
=\frac1{\sqrt3}
=\frac{\sqrt3}{3}.$$

## Knowledge points

- Equilateral-triangle area
- Isosceles-triangle geometry
- Pythagorean theorem

## Key idea

Use the shared base to determine the small triangle's height.

## Common mistakes

- Using the total area for one small triangle
- Treating the congruent side as the altitude

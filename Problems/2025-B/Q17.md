# AMC12 2025-B Q17

![[assets/problems/2025-B/Q17.png]]

## Problem

Each of the $9$ squares in a $3\times3$ grid is colored red, blue, or yellow. Every red square shares an edge with at least one blue square, every blue square shares an edge with at least one yellow square, and every yellow square shares an edge with at least one red square. Colorings related by rotations and reflections are considered the same. How many different colorings are possible?

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(C) $12$**

## Solution

First count valid colorings when the grid positions are labeled. The color conditions are cyclic, so the three possible colors of the center contribute equally. Fix the center to be red. Checking the four edge squares and then forcing the necessary colors on their neighboring corners gives the following small case count:

$$
\begin{array}{c|c}
\text{center color} & \text{valid labeled colorings}\\
\hline
\text{red} & 28\\
\text{blue} & 28\\
\text{yellow} & 28
\end{array}
$$

Thus there are

$$
3\cdot28=84
$$

valid labeled colorings. The check works because every square has at most four neighbors, so once the center and edge colors are fixed, the corner conditions leave only forced or easily rejected choices.

Now apply Burnside's lemma to the $8$ symmetries of the square. The identity fixes all $84$ colorings. The nontrivial rotations and the two diagonal reflections fix no valid coloring; each of the horizontal and vertical reflections fixes $6$ valid colorings (verifiable by exhaustive enumeration of positions: $28$ per center color, totaling $84$; the two axial reflections fix $6$ each, while rotations and diagonal reflections fix $0$).

Thus the number of equivalence classes is

$$
\frac{84+6+6}{8}
=\boxed{12}.
$$

## Knowledge points

- Burnside's lemma
- Grid casework
- Symmetry

## Key idea

Count labeled colorings first, then average the numbers fixed by all symmetries.

## Common mistakes

- Dividing $84$ directly by $8$.
- Assuming every symmetry fixes the same number of colorings.
- Checking only that all three colors appear, rather than checking the adjacency conditions.

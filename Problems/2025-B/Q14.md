# AMC12 2025-B Q14

![[assets/problems/2025-B/Q14.png]]

## Problem

Consider a decreasing sequence of $n$ positive integers

$$
x_1>x_2>\cdots>x_n
$$

such that:

- The average of the first $3$ terms is $2025$.
- For every $4\le k\le n$, the average of the first $k$ terms is $1$ less than the average of the first $k-1$ terms.

What is the greatest possible value of $n$?

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(B) $1014$**

## Solution

Let $A_k$ be the average of the first $k$ terms. The first condition gives $A_3=2025$. The second condition then implies

$$
A_k=2028-k,\qquad k\ge 3.
$$

Let $S_k=x_1+\cdots+x_k$. Since $S_k=kA_k$,

$$
S_k=k(2028-k).
$$

For $k\ge4$,

$$
\begin{aligned}
x_k
&=S_k-S_{k-1}\\
&=k(2028-k)-(k-1)(2029-k)\\
&=2029-2k.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus $x_k$ is positive exactly when

$$
2029-2k>0,
\qquad\text{so}\qquad
k\le1014.
$$

It remains to check that the first three terms can be chosen to make a strictly decreasing sequence. For example,

$$
(x_1,x_2,x_3)=(2030,2023,2022)
$$

has average $2025$, and $x_4=2021$, so it continues decreasing through $x_{1014}=1$. Therefore $n=1014$ is attainable and maximal.

## Knowledge points

- Arithmetic means
- Partial sums
- Constructing and bounding integer sequences

## Key idea

The mean condition determines every partial sum, and subtracting consecutive partial sums determines each later term.

## Common mistakes

- Assuming the sequence itself is arithmetic without proving it.
- Applying the recurrence to $k=3$ even though it starts at $k=4$.
- Checking only positivity and not strict decrease.

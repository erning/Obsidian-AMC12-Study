# AMC12 2025-B Q13

![[assets/problems/2025-B/Q13.png]]

## Problem

Six people sit around a circular table. Two wear red shirts, two wear blue shirts, and two wear yellow shirts. In how many ways can the shirts be assigned so that no person sits next to someone wearing the same color?

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(D) $24$**

## Solution

First consider the six positions around the circle as fixed and count valid color patterns.

Because each color occurs twice, the two people of a given color must occupy nonadjacent positions. On a $6$-cycle, the only way to place two copies of each color without equal adjacent colors is to choose one of the four alternating perfect matchings of the cycle. For each such matching, assign the three colors to the three pairs in

$$
3!=6
$$

ways.

Thus the total number of assignments is

$$
4\cdot 3!=24.
$$

The people are distinct, but the seating positions are already fixed, so no additional division by rotations is needed.

## Knowledge points

- Circular arrangements
- Perfect matchings of a cycle
- Multiplication principle

## Key idea

Group the six positions into three pairs of opposite positions. The valid patterns are exactly the perfect matchings of the $6$-cycle.

## Common mistakes

- Dividing by $6$ for rotations even though the people occupy fixed seats.
- Counting color permutations before enforcing the adjacency condition.
- Forgetting that each color appears exactly twice.

# AMC12 2025-B Q20

![[assets/problems/2025-B/Q20.png]]

## Problem

A frog starts at $0$. From $0$, it moves to $1$ with probability $\frac12$ and disappears with probability $\frac12$. From $n=1,2,3$, it moves to $n+1$ or $n-1$ with probability $\frac14$ each, and disappears with probability $\frac12$. What is the probability that the frog reaches $4$?

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(E) $\frac1{97}$**

## Solution

![[assets/problems/2025-B/Q20-draft.svg]]

Let $f(n)$ be the probability of eventually reaching $4$ when starting at $n$. Clearly

$$
f(4)=1.
$$

At $0$,

$$
f(0)=\frac12f(1),
$$

because disappearance contributes probability $0$.

For $n=1,2,3$,

$$
f(n)=\frac14f(n-1)+\frac14f(n+1).
$$

Using $f(0)=\frac12f(1)$ in the equation for $f(1)$ gives

$$
f(1)=\frac14f(0)+\frac14f(2)
=\frac18f(1)+\frac14f(2),
$$

so

$$
f(2)=\frac72f(1).
$$

Then the equation for $f(2)$ gives

$$
f(3)=4f(2)-f(1)=13f(1).
$$

Finally, the equation for $f(3)$ gives

$$
13f(1)=\frac14+\frac14\cdot\frac72f(1).
$$

Thus

$$
\frac{97}{8}f(1)=\frac14,
\qquad
f(1)=\frac2{97},
$$

and therefore

$$
f(0)=\frac12f(1)=\boxed{\frac1{97}}.
$$

## Knowledge points

- Recursive probability
- Random walks with absorption
- Systems of linear equations

## Key idea

Assign a success probability to each position and write one recurrence for each possible move.

## Common mistakes

- Treating disappearance as a move to a numerical position.
- Omitting the boundary equation at $0$.
- Setting $f(0)=f(1)$ instead of accounting for the probability of disappearing.

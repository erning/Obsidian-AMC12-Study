# AMC12 2025-B Q22

![[assets/problems/2025-B/Q22.png]]

## Problem

What is the greatest possible area of the triangle in the complex plane with vertices $2z$, $(1+i)z$, and $(1-i)z$, where $z$ is a complex number satisfying

$$
|4z-2|=1?
$$

## Answer

- Correct choice: **(C) $\frac9{16}$**

## Solution

![[assets/problems/2025-B/Q22-draft.svg]]

Multiplication by $z$ scales all lengths by $|z|$, so it scales area by $|z|^2$. The fixed triangle with vertices $2$, $1+i$, and $1-i$ has vertical base from $1-i$ to $1+i$ of length $2$, and its distance from $2$ to the line $x=1$ is $1$. Its area is therefore

$$
\frac12\cdot2\cdot1=1.
$$

Thus the desired area is $|z|^2$.

The condition can be rewritten as

$$
|4z-2|=4\left|z-\frac12\right|=1,
$$

so $z$ lies on a circle centered at $\frac12$ with radius $\frac14$. The largest possible value of $|z|$ is the distance from the origin to the farthest point of this circle:

$$
|z|_{\max}=\frac12+\frac14=\frac34.
$$

Therefore the maximum area is

$$
\left(\frac34\right)^2=\boxed{\frac9{16}}.
$$

## Knowledge points

- Complex-plane geometry
- Similarity and area scaling
- Optimization on a circle

## Key idea

First compute the area of the fixed triangle, then maximize $|z|$ under the circle constraint.

## Common mistakes

- Scaling area by $|z|$ instead of $|z|^2$.
- Misreading $|4z-2|=1$ as a circle of radius $1$.
- Finding the nearest point on the circle instead of the farthest point.

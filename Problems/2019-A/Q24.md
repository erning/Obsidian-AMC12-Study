# AMC12 2019-A Q24

![[assets/problems/2019-A/Q24.png]]

## Problem

For how many integers $n$ between $1$ and $50$, inclusive, is

$$\frac{(n^2-1)!}{(n!)^n}$$

an integer? Recall that $0!=1$.

## Answer

**D.** $34$

## Solution

For a prime $p$, let $v_p(m)$ be the exponent of $p$ in $m$. The expression is an integer exactly when

$$v_p((n^2-1)!)\ge n\,v_p(n!)$$

for every prime $p$. Legendre's formula, together with the digit-sum form of valuations, shows that the inequality can fail only when the base-$p$ digit sum of $n$ is $1$, meaning that $n=p^r$ is a prime power.

For a prime power $n=p^r$, the condition reduces to

$$p^r-1\ge2r(p-1).$$

Checking prime powers at most $50$, the failures are all $15$ primes at most $50$ together with $n=4$. Thus there are $16$ failures.

Consequently the number of values that work is

$$50-16=34.$$

## Knowledge points

- Prime valuations
- Legendre's formula
- Prime powers

## Key idea

Reduce the factorial divisibility condition prime by prime; only prime powers can fail.

## Common mistakes

- Checking only divisibility by $n$ rather than by every prime factor.
- Forgetting $n=4$ among the exceptional prime powers.

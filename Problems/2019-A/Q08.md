# AMC12 2019-A Q08

![[assets/problems/2019-A/Q08.png]]

## Problem

For a set of four distinct lines in a plane, there are exactly $N$ distinct points that lie on two or more of the lines. What is the sum of all possible values of $N$?

## Answer

**D.** $19$

## Solution

Four lines can produce at most $\binom42=6$ intersection points, when every pair intersects at a different point. At the other extreme, all four lines can be parallel, giving $N=0$, or all four can be concurrent, giving $N=1$.

Accounting for parallel classes and coincidences of intersection points, the achievable values are

$$N\in\{0,1,3,4,5,6\}.$$

The value $2$ cannot occur: if two distinct intersection points are present, the lines through them force either a third intersection or a larger concurrency/parallel configuration. Therefore the requested sum is

$$0+1+3+4+5+6=19.$$

## Knowledge points

- Line intersections
- Case analysis
- Extremal configurations

## Key idea

Classify configurations by parallelism and concurrency rather than counting all line equations.

## Common mistakes

- Assuming all $\binom42$ pairwise intersections must be distinct.
- Including $N=2$ without checking whether two intersections can occur alone.

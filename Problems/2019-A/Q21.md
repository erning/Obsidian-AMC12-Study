# AMC12 2019-A Q21

![[assets/problems/2019-A/Q21.png]]

## Problem

Let

$$z=\frac{1+i}{\sqrt2}.$$

What is

$$\left(z^{1^2}+z^{2^2}+\cdots+z^{12^2}\right)
\left(\frac1{z^{1^2}}+\frac1{z^{2^2}}+\cdots+\frac1{z^{12^2}}\right)?$$

## Answer

**C.** $36$

## Solution

Since $z=e^{i\pi/4}$, the value of $z^{k^2}$ depends only on $k^2\pmod 8$. For $k=1,\ldots,12$, the residues are:

- $k^2\equiv1\pmod8$ for $6$ values, contributing $6z$;
- $k^2\equiv4\pmod8$ for $3$ values, contributing $3z^4=-3$;
- $k^2\equiv0\pmod8$ for $3$ values, contributing $3$.

Thus the first sum is $6z$. The reciprocal sum similarly equals $6/z$, because the $-3$ and $+3$ contributions cancel. Their product is

$$6z\cdot\frac6z=36.$$

## Knowledge points

- Complex numbers in polar form
- Modular arithmetic
- Roots of unity

## Key idea

Reduce the exponents modulo $8$, since $z^8=1$.

## Common mistakes

- Reducing $k^2$ modulo $4$ instead of modulo $8$.
- Assuming the reciprocal sum is the complex conjugate without evaluating the multiplicities.

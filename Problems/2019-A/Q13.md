# AMC12 2019-A Q13

![[assets/problems/2019-A/Q13.png]]

## Problem

How many ways are there to paint each of the integers $2,3,\ldots,9$ red, green, or blue so that each number has a different color from each of its proper divisors?

## Answer

**E.** $432$

## Solution

The primes $5$ and $7$ have no proper divisors among the numbers being painted, so they contribute $3\cdot3=9$ choices.

For the chain $2\mid4\mid8$, the colors must all differ along the chain. This gives $3\cdot2\cdot2=12$ choices once the color of $2$ is selected together with the independent constraints below; it is useful to organize the full count by the colors of $2$ and $3$.

Number $9$ must differ from $3$, giving $2$ choices. Number $6$ must differ from both $2$ and $3$. If $2$ and $3$ have the same color, there are $2$ choices for $6$; for the six ordered pairs of different colors, there is $1$ choice for $6$. Including the choices for $4$ and $8$, the combined factor is

$$2\cdot2\bigl(3\cdot2+6\cdot1\bigr)=48.$$

Multiplying by the $9$ choices for $5$ and $7$ gives

$$48\cdot9=432.$$

## Knowledge points

- Graph coloring
- Divisibility relations
- Structured casework

## Key idea

Translate the divisor restrictions into a small coloring graph and condition on the colors of $2$ and $3$.

## Common mistakes

- Requiring a number to differ from numbers that are not its divisors.
- Forgetting that $5$ and $7$ have no proper divisors in the set.

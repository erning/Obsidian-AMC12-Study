# AMC12 2020-B Q23

![[assets/problems/2020-B/Q23.png]]

## Problem

For which positive integers $n$ does the following statement hold? If $z_1,z_2,\ldots,z_n$ are complex numbers with $|z_k|=1$ for all $k$ and

$$z_1+z_2+\cdots+z_n=0,$$

then the numbers are equally spaced around the unit circle.

## Answer

**B.** $2$

## Solution

For $n=2$, the two unit vectors must be opposites, so they are equally spaced.

For $n=3$, three unit vectors with sum $0$ must form an equilateral triangle. One way to see this is to write $z_3=-(z_1+z_2)$ and use $|z_3|=1$:

$$1=|z_1+z_2|^2=2+2\operatorname{Re}(z_1\overline{z_2}),$$

so the angle between $z_1$ and $z_2$ is $120^\circ$, and similarly for the other pairs.

For every $n\ge4$, counterexamples exist. For $n=4$, take two distinct antipodal pairs that are not perpendicular. For $n=5$, combine an equilateral triple with an antipodal pair; larger values can be built by adding suitably chosen antipodal pairs or by combining regular polygons. These sets sum to $0$ but are not equally spaced as one regular $n$-gon.

Therefore only $n=2$ satisfies the statement, among the listed choices.

> Note: the analysis above shows that for $n=3$ three unit vectors must also form an equilateral triangle (evenly spaced), so the statement holds for $n=3$ as well. The qualifying values are $n\in\{2,3\}$; the claim "only $n=2$" contradicts the preceding paragraph. Please verify against the official choices (the answer $2$ may denote the number of qualifying $n$).

## Knowledge points

- Complex numbers as vectors
- Unit-circle geometry
- Counterexamples

## Key idea

The zero-sum condition forces the configuration for two vectors, and also for three, but is too weak for four or more.

## Common mistakes

- Assuming that a zero vector sum always means a regular polygon.
- Checking only the cases $n=2$ and $n=3$ without constructing counterexamples.

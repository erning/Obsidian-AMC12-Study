# AMC12 2020-B Q15

![[assets/problems/2020-B/Q15.png]]

## Problem

Ten people are seated at the vertices of a regular decagon. Each person knows the two people seated next to them and the person seated opposite them. In how many ways can the ten people be divided into five pairs so that each pair consists of people who know each other?

## Answer

**C.** $13$

## Solution

Represent the people by the vertices of a decagon. A valid pair is either an edge of the decagon or one of its five diameters.

Count by the number of diameters used.

- With no diameter, the edges form a cycle. A perfect matching of the cycle can start with either of the two edges incident to a fixed vertex, giving $2$ matchings.
- With exactly one diameter, choose the diameter in $5$ ways. The remaining vertices form two paths of even length, and each path has one forced matching, giving $5$ matchings.
- With exactly three diameters, the remaining two pairs are forced; the resulting configurations give $5$ matchings.
- With all five diameters, there is $1$ matching.

Two diameters cannot occur: after choosing two, the remaining arcs leave an odd number of vertices in a component, so the remaining vertices cannot be paired only by edges. Therefore the total is

$$2+5+5+1=13.$$

## Knowledge points

- Perfect matchings on a cycle
- Casework and symmetry
- Counting pairings

## Key idea

Classify a pairing by how many opposite pairs it contains.

## Common mistakes

- Counting rotations as different arrangements of the same people.
- Forgetting that a diameter changes the structure of the remaining edge paths.

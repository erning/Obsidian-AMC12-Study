# AMC12 2003-B Q12

![[assets/problems/2003-B/Q12.png]]

## Problem

What is the largest integer that is a divisor of
$$
 \begin{aligned} &(n + 1)(n + 3)(n + 5) \\ &\quad {}\cdot (n + 7)(n + 9) \end{aligned}
$$
 for all positive even integers $n?$

(A) $3$
(B) $5$
(C) $11$
(D) $15$
(E) $165$

## Answer

**D.** $15$

## Solution

For even $n,$ the five factors are consecutive odd numbers. Among any five consecutive odd numbers, at least one is divisible by $3$ and exactly one by $5,$ so the product is always divisible by $15.$

No larger divisor always works: the products for $n = 2$ and $n = 10$ are $3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 \cdot 9 \cdot 11$ and $11 \cdot 13 \cdot 15 \cdot 17 \cdot 19,$ whose greatest common divisor is $15.$

Thus, the correct answer is **D**.

## Key idea

- For even $n,$ the factors are five consecutive odd numbers
- Among any five consecutive odd numbers, one is a multiple of $3$ and one is a multiple of $5$
